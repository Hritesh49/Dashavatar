MATSYA AVATAR

The first avatar of Lord Vishnu in the form of a Fish. Matsya is described to have rescued the first man, Manu, from a great deluge. Matsya may be depicted as a giant fish, often golden in color, or anthropomorphically with the torso of Vishnu connected to the rear half of a fish.

KURMA AVATAR

The second avatar of Lord Vishnu in the form of a Tortoise. in this avatar, he was involved in the extraction of immortality liquid and is depicted in the water holding up Mount Mandāra, chosen as a churning rod upon which Vishnu himself is seated.

VARAHA AVATAR

The third avatar of Lord Vishnu in the form of a Wild Boar. Varaha is most commonly associated with the legend of lifting the earth (personified as the goddess Bhudevi) out of the cosmic ocean. Varaha slew the asura Hiranyaksha, and retrieved the earth from the ocean, lifting it on his tusks.

NARASHIMA AVATAR

The fourth avatar of Lord Vishnu in the form of a Man Lion. He is regarded to have incarnated in the form of a part-lion, part-man (Narasingha) being to slay Hiranyakashipu, to end religious persecution and calamity on earth, thereby restoring dharma.

VAMANA AVATAR

The fifth avatar of Lord Vishnu in the form of a Dwarf. Vamana is most commonly associated in the Hindu epics and Puranas with the legend of taking back the three worlds (Triloka) from the daitya-king Bali by taking three steps to restore the cosmic order.

LORD PARASHURAMA

The sixth avatar of Lord Vishnu, Parashurama. He corrected the cosmic equilibrium by destroying the Kshatriya warriors twenty-one times. He is believed to be one of the Chiranjeevis (Immortals), who will appear at the end of the Kali Yuga to be the guru of Vishnu's last incarnation, Kalki.

LORD RAMA

The seventh avatar of Lord Vishnu, Rama Chandra. Rama was born to Kaushalya and Dasharatha in Ayodhya, the capital of the Kingdom of Kosala. He married Sita. In this avata, he killed Ravan. The entire life story of Rama, Sita and their companions allegorically discusses duties, rights and social responsibilities of an individual. It illustrates dharma and dharmic living through model characters.

SHRI KRISHNA

The eighth avatar of Lord Vishnu, Shree Krishna. He is the god of protection, compassion, tenderness, and love. The anecdotes and narratives of Krishna's life are generally titled as Krishna Līlā. He is a central character in the Mahabharata, the Bhagavata Purana, the Brahma Vaivarta Purana, and the Bhagavad Gita. His iconography reflects these legends, and shows him in different stages of his life, such as an infant eating butter, a young boy playing a flute, a young boy with Radha or surrounded by female devotees; or a friendly charioteer giving counsel to Arjuna.

GAUTAMA BUDDHA

The ninth avatar of Lord Vishnu, Gautama Buddha. The Buddha has been a formative force in the origins of Hinduism. According to the Agni Purana, Vishnu assumed this incarnation on earth due to the daityas defeating the devas in their battles. In order to restore the natural order, he deluded the asuras with his teachings. This caused them to abandon the path established by the Vedas and convert to Buddhism, causing them to be devoid of dharma.

KALKI AVATAR

The tenth and last avatar of Lord Vishnu, Kalki. He is described as the incarnation who appears at the end of the Kali Yuga. He ends the darkest, degenerating, and chaotic stage of the Kali Yuga (Period) to remove adharma and ushers in the Satya Yuga, while riding a white horse with a fiery sword. He restarts a new cycle of time. He is described as a Brahmin warrior in the Puranas.
